> "He says, 'Be still, and know that I am God; I will be exalted among the nations, I will be exalted in the earth.'"  
> — **Psalm 46:10**

Your brain has not had a quiet moment in a long time. From the second you wake up, notifications, podcasts, music, and group chats pour into your ears. We have engineered a world where silence feels uncomfortable, almost threatening. But the soul was not built to process that much input, and it is starving for the stillness it was designed for.

Silence is not empty. It is where you finally hear the things you have been drowning out. The guilt you have been ignoring. The joy you have been too distracted to feel. The voice of God, which rarely shouts over the noise but speaks clearly when the noise is gone. Elijah did not hear God in the wind or the earthquake, but in a whisper.

Building silence into your day does not require a monastery. It requires honesty about what you are using noise to avoid. Try ten minutes in the morning without your phone. Drive to work without a podcast. Sit on your porch at night and just listen. It will feel awkward at first. Stay with it.

This week, schedule one fifteen-minute block of intentional quiet. Put your phone in another room. Do not pray with words, do not read, do not plan. Just sit. Let God meet you in the space you usually fill. That silence is not wasted time. It is the soil where real faith grows.

## Taking It Further

Knowing truth is only half the battle. Living it is where the rubber meets the road. So what does this look like in your life this week? It might mean having a hard conversation you've been avoiding. It might mean writing down a promise and reading it every morning before your feet hit the floor. It might mean simply pausing at the end of the day to ask one honest question: Did I walk this out today?

Scripture is a living word — and it changes lives when we put it into practice. Don't let today's reading stay on the page. Let it travel with you into every conversation, every decision, every quiet moment. That's when the gospel stops being information and starts being transformation. And that's the kind of faith worth rocking on your sleeve — literally.
